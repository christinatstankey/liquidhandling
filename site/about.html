About

The problem

Safety Data Sheets tell you a reagent is flammable, irritating, or hazardous. They don't tell your liquid handler to pre-wet tips three times before aspirating ethanol, or to warm DMSO to room temperature before it becomes a solid plug in your tip.

That knowledge lives in the heads of experienced bench scientists. It's passed on verbally, buried in methods sections, or learned by ruining an experiment.

What this does

This is a transparent rules layer between chemical properties and robot instructions. Each reagent has a structured property profile — vapor pressure, viscosity, light sensitivity, and so on. A declarative rules engine maps those properties to handling instructions. Every rule cites a property value or a published source. Nothing is hidden inside a model.

The output is a JSON handling profile a liquid handler can consume directly: aspiration speed, tip type, pre-wet cycles, container type, storage conditions, safety requirements.

What it isn't

This is not an SDS database, not an EH&S compliance tool, and not a general protocol library. It is a narrow, high-fidelity bridge between physicochemical properties and robot-actionable handling decisions, scoped to reagents on a stockroom shelf.

Sources and honesty

Every rule is cited. Where a rule reflects common bench practice without a clean primary source, it is flagged explicitly as field consensus in the UI. The goal is to surface uncertainty, not hide it.

Data sourced from manufacturer SDS sheets and published bench protocols.
